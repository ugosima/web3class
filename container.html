LESSON 2: Intro to Blockchain ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is Blockchain Technology?


Blockchain technology is one of the most revolutionary innovations of the 21st century, powering digital currencies like Bitcoin and transforming industries with its promise of transparency, security, and decentralization; But what exactly is blockchain?

At its core, a blockchain is a digital ledger like a database, that stores information in a secure and tamper-proof way. Unlike traditional databases controlled by a single entity (like a bank or company), blockchain is decentralized, meaning it is shared across many computers or nodes.

Each entry in this ledger is called a block, and these blocks are linked or "chained" together in chronological order‚Äîhence the name blockchain.


How Blockchain Works

  1. Transaction Occurs: A user initiates a transaction, such as sending cryptocurrency or recording data.
  2. Transaction is Verified: A network of computers (called nodes) validates the transaction using algorithms.
  3. Transaction is Added to a Block: Once verified, the transaction is grouped with others into a block.
  4. Block is Added to the Chain: The block is attached to the existing blockchain in a permanent, unchangeable way.

Once a block is added, the data it contains is nearly impossible to alter without changing every subsequent block‚Äîa feature that makes blockchain highly secure.


💡 Analogy: How Blockchain Works


Imagine a classroom where every student has a notebook. This notebook is used to record every time someone borrows or returns a book from the class library.

üìò Step-by-Step Analogy:

  1. A Transaction Happens: John borrows a book titled "Science Today".
  2. Everyone Sees It: Instead of one person writing it down, all students copy the same transaction into their notebooks:
    "John borrowed 'Science Today' at 10:00 AM"
  3. Information is Locked In: Once all students write it down, that page in the notebook is considered complete and can't be changed.
  4. The Chain Continues: The next transaction (e.g., Mary returns "Math Basics") is recorded on a new page, following the previous one.

This way, the class keeps a tamper-proof, shared history of every library transaction. If someone tries to cheat and change their notebook, it won‚Äôt match others‚Äîand everyone will know!

üîí How This Relates to Blockchain:

  • The notebook represents the blockchain.
  • The students are like the network nodes (computers).
  • The recorded entries are the blocks.
  • The linking of pages is the chain of blocks.
  • Consensus among students ensures no one can fake or alter history.

In summary: Just like the classroom notebook system, blockchain creates a shared, secure, and permanent record of transactions‚Äîopen to all, but impossible to alter without agreement from everyone involved.


Key Features of Blockchain

  • Decentralization: No single authority controls the blockchain. All participants have access to the same information.
  • Transparency: Transactions are visible to all participants in the network.
  • Security: Blockchain uses cryptographic algorithms to ensure data is secure and cannot be tampered with.
  • Immutability: Once data is added to the blockchain, it cannot be changed or deleted.
  • Consensus Mechanism: Decisions are made collectively by the network, usually through methods like Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS).

Applications of Blockchain

Blockchain is not limited to cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin or Ethereum. It has a wide range of applications across various sectors:

  • Finance: Enabling peer-to-peer payments, eliminating the need for middlemen, and increasing transaction speed.
  • Healthcare: Storing patient records securely and giving patients control over their data.
  • Supply Chain: Tracking products from source to destination, ensuring authenticity and quality.
  • Voting Systems: Creating tamper-proof digital voting systems to improve transparency and trust.
  • Real Estate: Recording property ownership and transaction history in a secure manner.

Advantages of Blockchain

  • Reduces fraud and corruption
  • Improves efficiency and speed of transactions
  • Lowers operational costs by eliminating intermediaries
  • Empowers users with control over their own data

Challenges of Blockchain

  • Scalability: Some blockchains are slow and cannot handle a large number of transactions.
  • Energy Consumption: Proof of Work systems can use massive amounts of electricity.
  • Regulatory Uncertainty: Many governments are still deciding how to regulate blockchain technology.
  • Complexity: The technology is still new and difficult for many to understand or implement.

Conclusion

Blockchain technology has the potential to transform industries by making systems more secure, transparent, and decentralized. While it comes with its own set of challenges, its advantages are far-reaching. As innovation continues, blockchain is likely to become an even more significant part of our digital future.

Understanding blockchain today means being ready for tomorrow‚Äôs world of technology and finance.

LESSON 3: Introduction to Cryptocurrency ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In the digital age, the concepts of cryptography and cryptocurrency are becoming increasingly important, especially as the world moves toward decentralized systems, blockchain-based platforms, and digital finance. While they are often mentioned together, they are not the same thing; However, they are deeply connected.


What is Cryptography?

Cryptography is the science of protecting data through mathematical techniques. It's goal is to secure communication and ensure that only intended recipients can understand the message or access the data.


Core Functions of Cryptography

Cryptography serves four main functions that are crucial for secure communication and data integrity, these are:

  1. Confidentiality : Prevents unauthorized access to information.
  2. Integrity : Ensures the information hasn‚Äôt been altered.
  3. Authentication : Verifies the identity of users or sources.
  4. Non-repudiation : Prevents denial of involvement in a transaction.

Types of Cryptography

  1. Symmetric Encryption: The same key is used to encrypt and decrypt data.
  2. Asymmetric Encryption: Uses a public and private key pair.

Symmetric encryption is faster and suitable for large data, while asymmetric encryption is more secure and used for key exchange and digital signatures. It is important to note that both types of cryptography are essential for the functioning of cryptocurrencies, as they provide the necessary security and trust mechanisms.

What is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is a type of digital or virtual currency that uses cryptography to secure transactions, control the creation of new units, and validate asset transfers.



Relationship between Cryptography and Cryptocurrency

Cryptography is what makes cryptocurrency possible. Without cryptography, cryptocurrencies would be easy to counterfeit, vulnerable to fraud, and impossible to verify. Therefore, cryptography provides the foundation for the security, integrity, and trustworthiness of cryptocurrencies.

Cryptography makes cryptocurrencies possible by the implementation of several key concepts and technologies. Here‚Äôs how they work together and the benefits of cryptography:

1. Digital Signatures

When someone sends cryptocurrency, they must prove ownership of the funds using a private key.

  • A digital signature is created using cryptographic algorithms.
  • It is verified by others using the sender‚Äôs public key.
    • This ensures authenticity without revealing the private key, It's like signing a check, but mathematically, and in a way that can‚Äôt be forged.

2. Hash Functions

A hash function turns input into a fixed-length string. It is one-way and impossible to reverse.

Even small changes in input create vastly different outputs.

Use Cases :

  1. Transaction IDs
  2. Block verification
  3. Proof-of-Work (e.g., mining)
    4. This special functions are only possible through cryptography.

3. Wallets and Key Pairs

A crypto wallet stores your keys, not your money.

  1. Public Key: Like a bank account number
  2. Private Key: Like your ATM PIN
    3. This special keys are generated using cryptographic algorithms, ensuring that only the owner can access their funds.

4. Secure Transactions on the Blockchain

Each crypto transaction is encrypted, signed, and verified using cryptographic functions. No banks are needed; the network itself is the trust layer.


5. Consensus Mechanisms Use Cryptography

Cryptocurrencies use consensus to agree on transactions:

  1. Proof of Work (PoW) ‚Äì Mining with computational puzzles
  2. Proof of Stake (PoS) ‚Äì Validators hold coins to propose blocks

Examples of cryptocurrencies, and cryptographic applications

  1. Bitcoin & Ethereum ‚Äì Core cryptographic networks
  2. NFTs ‚Äì Verified with cryptographic proofs
  3. zk-SNARKs ‚Äì Enables private transactions
  4. Signal & Secure Messaging ‚Äì Uses cryptographic protocols

Challenges of Cryptocurrency

  1. Key Loss :     Lose your key, lose access
  2. User Error :   ‚Äì No support to recover lost funds
  3. Scalability :     ‚Äì Some blockchains are slow and requires enormous resources to scale
  4. Quantum Threat :     ‚Äì May break current encryption

Summary

Cryptography and cryptocurrency are inseparable. One is the science of security, and the other is the application of that science to money. Together, they enable a new era of digital finance where individuals control their assets.

LESSON 5: Beginner Terminologies in cryptocurrency. ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cryptocurrency can seem like a foreign language when you're just starting out. From blockchains and wallets to gas fees and HODL, the crypto world is full of unique terms of which understanding them is essential for anyone looking to get involved with cryptocurrency, whether as a user, investor, developer, or enthusiast.

Let's look at some of the most common and important terminologies in cryptocurrency,all explained in simple terms.

1. Blockchain/Network

A blockchain is a digital ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers. Each group of transactions forms a "block," and these blocks are linked together in a chain. It is the foundational technology behind cryptocurrencies, every blockchain has it's coin.


2. Coin or Token

A Coin or Token is any digital form of money that is decentralized and built on blockchain technology. Examples include Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Solana. Coins are made specifically for the blockchain, while Tokens are crypto built on an already existing blockchain.

Note:Though they are not the same, Token/Coin are used interchangeably to mean the same thing in practise.


3. Wallet

A wallet is a software or hardware tool used to store, send, and receive cryptocurrencies, it is similar to a bank account. It contains your public and private keys, and your wallet address all generated from your seed phrase.

  1. Hot Wallet: Connected to the internet, e.g. Metamask, Trustwallet, Phantom e.t.c.
  2. Cold Wallet: Offline, more secure e.g., hardware wallets like Ledger, they are usually specialized devices for crypto transactions.

4. Seed phrase / Recovery Phrase

This is the 12 or 24 words phrase that gives access to a wallets private key, everybody that has this words in the right order will access the funds in the wallet, so it is meant to be protected and must not be revealed to 2nd party; it is recommended that they are handwritten on paper and filed up safely, losing them also means losing access to your account.


5. Private Key / Public Key

Private and public keys are unique cryptographic keys used in blockchain transactions, they are generated from seed phrases and are uniquely bound to a particular seed phrase. They are essential for securing your wallet and authorizing transactions.

Never share your private key with anyone, as it gives full control over your wallet and funds. If someone has your private key, they can access and transfer your cryptocurrency without your permission.

Simply put :

  1. Public Key: Shared openly, and can be used at the backend of dapps to generate your wallet address.
  2. Private Key: Kept secret and used to authorize transactions. Losing or exposing this means losing the sole access and ownership to your funds.
Note : A public key is not a wallet address, it is a longer string of characters that is used to generate the wallet address; do not transfer funds to a public key.


6. Wallet Address

A crypto address is a unique string of characters used to send or receive cryptocurrency, similar to a bank account number.

It is derived from your public key and can be shared with others to receive funds; Always double-check the address before sending crypto, as transactions are irreversible, to prevent mistakes, always copy and paste addresses, do not type them.

Example : A Bitcoin address looks like this: 1A1zP1eP5QGefi2DMPTfTL5SLmv7DivfNa

Note : Always ensure you are using the correct address format for the specific cryptocurrency you are dealing with, as different coins have different address formats; you will develop this knowledge with time.


7. Gas Fee

A gas fee is a small payment required to perform a transaction or execute a smart contract on a blockchain. It pays the network validators for processing.


8. Gas Limit

The gas limit is the maximum amount of gas you are willing to spend on a transaction. If the transaction requires more gas than the limit, it will fail.


9. DApp (Decentralized Application)

A DApp is an application that runs on a decentralized network, typically using smart contracts. DApps are open-source, operate autonomously, and are not controlled by a single entity.


10. Faucet

A faucet is a website or app that gives out small amounts of cryptocurrency for free, usually to help users learn how to use wallets and transactions. It is often used to distribute testnet coins for developers and testers.


11. Testnet

A testnet is a separate blockchain used for testing purposes. It allows developers to experiment with new features without risking real money. Testnets use test coins that have no real value.


12. Exchange

A cryptocurrency exchange is a platform where you can buy, sell, or trade cryptocurrencies. They are grouped into :

  1. Centralized Exchange (CEX): Operated by a company, requires registration, and holds user funds; e.g Binance, Coinbase, Gate.io,MEXC, e.t.c
  2. Decentralized Exchange (DEX): Peer-to-peer trading without intermediaries, users retain control of their funds; e.g Jupiter, Uniswap, Pancakeswap, e.t.c
  3. Hybrid Exchange: Combines features of both CEX and DEX, offering a balance of security and usability; e.g. Kado, dYdX, e.t.c

13. Altcoin

Any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin is called an altcoin (alternative coin). Examples include Ethereum, Litecoin, and Solana.


14. Whitepaper

A whitepaper is a document that explains the purpose, technology, and plans behind a cryptocurrency or blockchain project. Bitcoin‚Äôs whitepaper was published by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2008.


15. Token Symbol & Decimals

Every cryptocurrency has a ticker used to identify the token e.g, Bitcoin's ticker is BTC, Ethereum is ETH, and Solana is SOL.


16. Tokenomics

Short for "Token Economics", refers to the economic model behind a token i.e how it is created, distributed, used, and maintained to give it value.


17. Total Supply

The Maximum number of a token that will ever created.


18. Circulating Supply

The total number of Tokens currently in public circulation.


19. Initial Supply

The Total number of the Tokens released at launch.


20. Market Cap

Market capitalization is calculated as price of a coin multiplied by the total number of coins in circulation. It is used to measure the size and popularity of a cryptocurrency.


21. Burning

This is the act of permanently removing tokens to reduce supply, it is done by sending tokens to a non-existent or non-accessible address; burning creates scarcity, thereby increasing the value of a crypto token.


22. Emission Rate

Emission rate is a term used to describe the rate at which new tokens are created and introduced into circulation, It may be per block, per day, or per year; It is an important aspect of tokenomics, influencing supply and demand dynamics.


23. Mining

Mining is the process of verifying transactions and adding them to the blockchain. It is mostly used in Proof of Work systems like Bitcoin. Miners are rewarded with new cryptocurrency for their work.


24. Proof of Work (PoW) vs Proof of Stake (PoS)

  • Proof of Work (PoW): A consensus mechanism where miners solve complex mathematical problems to validate transactions and secure the network. It requires significant computational power and energy.
  • Proof of Stake (PoS): A consensus mechanism where validators are chosen to create new blocks based on the number of coins they hold and are willing to "stake" as collateral. It is more energy-efficient than PoW.

    • 25. Staking

    Staking involves locking up cryptocurrency in a wallet to support the operations of a blockchain network. It is used in Proof of Stake systems, and stakers are rewarded with additional coins.


    26. Volatility

    Volatility refers to how much and how quickly the price of a cryptocurrency changes. Crypto is known for being highly volatile.


    27. FUD

    FUD stands for Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt. It is a term used when negative news or rumors affect the price of a cryptocurrency, often causing panic selling.


    28. HODL

    Originally a typo of "hold", HODL means "Hold On for Dear Life". It encourages crypto holders to keep their coins long-term despite market drops.


    29. Pump and Dump

    A scheme where the price of a cryptocurrency is artificially inflated (pumped) and then quickly sold off (dumped) for profit. This often affects small or low-volume coins.


    30. Bear Market / Bull Market

    1. Bull Market: Prices are rising, and optimism is high.
    2. Bear Market: Prices are falling, and pessimism dominates.

    31. NFT (Non-Fungible Token)

    A type of digital asset that represents ownership of unique items like art, music, or game collectibles. NFTs are mostly built on Ethereum.


    32. Airdrops

    An airdrop is a method of rewarding the holders or participants of a specific cryptocurrency with that crypto token. It is often used to promote new projects or reward loyal users.

    Airdrops are used to raise awareness about a new project or to incentivize users to engage with a platform.

    To receive an airdrop, you usually need to hold a certain amount of a specific cryptocurrency in your wallet at a given time, or you may need to complete certain tasks like following social media accounts, joining a community, or helping in the development of the project.

    Note: Always be cautious of airdrops, as some may be scams or require you to share sensitive information. Legitimate airdrops will never ask for your private key or seed phrase.


    33. ICO / IDO

    1. ICO: Initial Coin Offering ‚Äî a fundraising method where a new cryptocurrency is sold to early investors.
    2. IDO: Initial DEX Offering ‚Äî a similar process launched on decentralized exchanges.
